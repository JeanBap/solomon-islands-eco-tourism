Project profile, Maldives

Reefscapers.

Coral propagation across 30+ Maldivian resorts since 2001. Sponsored coral frames, guest out-planting, and a flagship lab at Four Seasons Landaa Giraavaru in Baa Atoll.

The short version. Reefscapers is the largest coral propagation programme in the Maldives by partner-resort count and tracked-frame count. Founded in 2001 by Marine Savers' Thomas Le Berre, it operates a frame-based propagation model where coral fragments are wired to steel frames, deployed in resort-house reefs, and tracked over time using photogrammetry and dive surveys [1] [2].

Methodology: coral frames

Reefscapers uses cylindrical or hemispherical steel frames coated with marine epoxy [1]. Coral fragments collected from broken or stressed colonies (and during opportunistic salvage events) are attached using cable ties. Frames are weighted and deployed at depths of 3 to 12 metres on resort-house reefs. Each frame is photographed quarterly, recording survival, growth, and bleaching events. The result is one of the longest-running coral-restoration datasets in the central Indian Ocean.

Sponsored frames and guest engagement

Resort guests can sponsor a coral frame, attach name plaques, and receive periodic photographic updates. This funds the operational cost of the programme and adds a personal component to the engagement. Many partner resorts integrate frame sponsorship into honeymoon, anniversary, and corporate retreat packages [3].

Partner resorts

Reefscapers' largest concentration of frames is at the Four Seasons Maldives properties (Landaa Giraavaru in Baa, Kuda Huraa in North Malé, and the private island Voavah). Additional partner resorts span all major atolls. The full list is published on the Reefscapers website and is regularly updated [1]. Notable partners include Soneva resorts in Baa and Noonu, Six Senses Laamu, Kuramathi, several Anantara properties, and a number of Maldives-based dive operators.

Results to date

Reefscapers reports over 7,000 individual coral frames deployed across the Maldives [1]. Survival outcomes are bleaching-event sensitive: the 2016 Indian Ocean bleaching event caused significant mortality across the Maldivian reef, including frames in shallow zones, while frames in deeper or thermally refuged sites showed higher survival. Specific peer-reviewed assessments of frame-based propagation in the Maldives are summarised in Becker et al. (2021) and Montano et al. (2022) [4] [5].

Limitations and caveats

  • Restoration is not a substitute for climate action. Reefscapers explicitly frames its work as supplementary to mitigation, not as a stand-alone solution.
  • Frame restoration favours fast-growing branching corals. The species mix on frames is therefore not representative of natural reef community composition.
  • Cost per coral. Resort-funded frame restoration is more expensive per coral than larval-based methods being trialled by SECORE and others. Reefscapers acknowledges this tradeoff and presents the visitor-engagement value as part of the programme's purpose.
