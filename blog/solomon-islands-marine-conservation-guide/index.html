Solomon Islands marine conservation: the definitive guide.

A full reference for the Solomon Islands and surrounding Coral Triangle. Best eco lodges, active conservation projects, lodge plus experience combinations, season-by-season diving, regional comparisons, and a 20-question long-tail FAQ. Verified primary sources throughout.

Quick answer The Solomon Islands sit at the heart of the Coral Triangle, the global epicentre of marine biodiversity. The country offers a small, deliberate set of premium-eco lodges (Uepi Island Resort, Tavanipupu, Sanbis, Fatboys, Papatura, Tetepare Eco Lodge), a community-led conservation model anchored by the Tetepare Descendants Association and the Arnavon Community Marine Conservation Area, and reef diving widely rated among the world's best. Year-round average sea temperature is 28 to 30 degrees Celsius and the optimum dive season runs April to November.

The state of marine conservation in the Solomon Islands

The Solomon Islands archipelago lies in the south-western Pacific, immediately east of Papua New Guinea and north of Vanuatu. The country is a member of the Coral Triangle Initiative on Coral Reefs, Fisheries and Food Security (CTI-CFF), the six-nation partnership that defines the global epicentre of marine biodiversity [1]. Coral Triangle waters host approximately 76 percent of the world's reef-building coral species and 37 percent of all reef fish species [2]. Solomon Islands sits firmly within this band.

Country-level marine biodiversity figures: Allen and Erdmann (2008) and subsequent surveys document over 1,000 reef fish species in Solomon Islands waters, more than 485 species of hard coral, 30 species of mangrove, and resident populations of dugong, hawksbill turtle, green turtle, leatherback turtle, and saltwater crocodile [3] [4]. The Khaled bin Sultan Living Oceans Foundation Global Reef Expedition baseline survey of 2014 covered Western, Isabel, and Choiseul Provinces and remains the most comprehensive single-country reef baseline available [5].

Threats are real and named honestly. Coral bleaching from rising sea-surface temperature has affected reefs across the country, with the major regional bleaching event of 2016 reaching Solomon waters [6]. Logging-driven sediment runoff from terrestrial concessions damages adjacent reefs in several provinces, particularly Choiseul, Isabel, and parts of Western Province. Localised blast and cyanide fishing has been reported historically though enforcement under the Solomon Islands Fisheries Management Act has reduced visible incidents [7]. Climate-driven sea-level rise threatens low-lying atolls in Temotu and Western Province communities.

Recent conservation wins are concrete. The Arnavon Community Marine Conservation Area, established in 1995 by Lauru, Katupika, and Wagina communities with The Nature Conservancy, has produced documented recovery of hawksbill turtle nesting numbers across three decades [8]. The Tetepare Descendants Association, formed in 2002, manages the world's largest uninhabited tropical island as a community no-take zone, with ranger patrols funded directly by eco lodge revenue [9]. East Rennell was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1998, the first natural property in the Solomons [10]. Marovo Lagoon, the world's largest saltwater lagoon, has been on the UNESCO Tentative World Heritage List since 2008 [11]. The Solomon Islands Locally Managed Marine Areas (SILMMA) network provides national-level coordination across community managed sites in every province.

The defining feature of Solomon Islands conservation is community ownership. Unlike Pacific neighbours where major protected areas are gazetted by national governments, almost every Solomon Islands marine protected area is managed by the customary landowners themselves. This produces both strengths (durable local accountability, intergenerational continuity) and gaps (uneven enforcement capacity, dependence on external funding for ranger costs).

Lodges ranked by category

Solomon Islands premium-eco lodging is small-scale and intentional. There are no Aman properties, no overwater villa megaresorts. The lodges below were selected against verified criteria: published TripAdvisor and dive media ratings of 4.5+, recognition by Lonely Planet Solomon Islands or Frommer's Solomon Islands as "top picks", or featured spotlight coverage in industry publications including Diver, Sport Diver, or Asian Diver in the past five years.

Top 5 luxury lodges

1. Uepi Island Resort

Marovo Lagoon, Western Province | 4-star equivalent | A$650 to A$900 per person per night

The flagship Solomon Islands eco lodge. Private island on the Marovo barrier reef with a house reef rated by Diver Magazine among the best on Earth. Hammerheads, dogtooth tuna, manta ray, and grey reef shark all in regular rotation [12]. Restoration partner: Coral Sea Foundation reef monitoring trips depart from Uepi. Best season: April to November. Get there: Solomon Airlines Honiara to Seghe (45 min) plus 1 hr boat to Uepi.

2. Tavanipupu Island Resort

Marau Sound, Guadalcanal Province | 4-star | A$700 to A$1,000 per person per night

Private island in calm Marau Sound on Guadalcanal's east coast. Famously hosted Will and Kate during their 2012 Pacific tour. Calm protected lagoon ideal for non-divers, snorkellers, and families. Restoration partner: Marau Sound community managed marine area. Best season: year-round with April to November optimal. Get there: 45-minute light aircraft from Honiara.

3. Sanbis Resort

Lola Island, Vona Vona Lagoon, Western Province | 4-star equivalent | A$450 to A$700 per person per night

Boutique resort on Lola Island in Vona Vona Lagoon. Twelve overwater bungalows. WWII wreck diving, manta sites, and reef diving within 20 minutes of the dock. Restoration partner: WWF Solomons Western Province MPAs. Best season: April to November. Get there: Solomon Airlines Honiara to Munda (90 min) plus 30-minute boat.

4. Fatboys Resort

Mbabanga Island, Vona Vona Lagoon, Western | 3.5-star with overwater bungalows | A$400 to A$600 per person per night

Distinctive overwater bungalow eco lodge on Mbabanga, near Gizo. The Reef Bar restaurant sits on a stilted platform 100 metres into the lagoon. Restoration partner: WWF Solomons community programmes. Best season: April to November. Get there: Solomon Airlines Honiara to Gizo (90 min) plus 25-minute boat.

5. Papatura Island Retreat

Isabel Province | Boutique | A$500 to A$750 per person per night

Private island on Santa Isabel's north coast. Closest premium lodge to the Arnavon CMCA (approximately 3-hour boat across the Manning Strait, weather permitting). Renowned for surfing as well as reef diving. Restoration partner: Arnavon Community Marine Conservation Area boat day-trips. Best season: April to October. Get there: Solomon Airlines Honiara to Buala (1 hr) plus 90-minute boat.

Top 5 dive-focused lodges and operators

1. Uepi Island Resort

Marovo Lagoon, Western Province

Dual citation: also our top luxury choice. The house reef alone justifies the trip. PADI 5-star Dive Resort. Specialty courses including Coral Sea Foundation reef monitoring training [12].

2. Dive Munda

Munda, Roviana Lagoon, Western

The country's leading independent dive operator. Specialises in WWII wreck diving (Munda was a major Pacific theatre airfield), reef diving, and Roviana Lagoon access. Pairs with Agnes Gateway Hotel or Munda Coastal Lodge for accommodation [13].

3. Bilikiki Cruises (MV Bilikiki)

Western, Russell, Florida Islands liveaboard

The longest-running purpose-built dive liveaboard in the Solomon Islands, operating since 1989. 10 to 12-night charters covering Russell Islands, Florida Islands, Marovo Lagoon, and Munda Bay. Six master cabins, two deluxe [14].

4. Sanbis Resort dive centre

Lola Island, Vona Vona

In-house PADI 5-star Dive Resort. Wreck diving on the Toa Maru (Japanese transport, sunk 1942) and reef diving across Vona Vona Lagoon.

5. Tulagi Dive (Solomon Diving)

Tulagi, Florida Islands, Central Province

Wreck diving capital of the Solomons. The 1942 to 1943 Battle of Guadalcanal left dozens of wrecks in Iron Bottom Sound, several within recreational dive depth. Tulagi Dive operates day boats and overnight stays [15].

Top 5 community / indigenous-owned lodges

1. Tetepare Eco Lodge

Tetepare Island, Western Province | Community-owned

Run directly by the Tetepare Descendants Association. Six basic-but-beautiful traditional bungalows. Revenue funds the TDA ranger programme. Tetepare is the world's largest uninhabited tropical island in protected status [9]. Best for: travellers committed to community-funded conservation.

2. Titiana Eco Lodge

Gizo, Western Province | Community-aligned

Small village-aligned lodge near Gizo. Direct support to local fishing families. Pairs cleanly with Dive Gizo or Adventure Sports Solomon Islands.

3. Arnavon Camp (visitor accommodation)

Arnavon Islands, between Isabel and Choiseul | Community-managed

Basic ranger camp accommodation by community arrangement during hawksbill nesting research season. Limited capacity. Verify with The Nature Conservancy Solomon Islands country office before assuming availability.

4. Roviana Lagoon homestays

Roviana villages, Western Province | Village-run

Multiple village homestay programmes operate within the Roviana and Vonavona Lagoon community managed marine area. WWF Solomons and the Roviana Conservation Foundation can provide current contact details.

5. Marovo Lagoon village homestays

Marovo Lagoon, Western Province | Village-run

Across the Marovo Lagoon UNESCO Tentative World Heritage area, multiple village homestays accept guests by direct arrangement [11]. Pairs naturally with Uepi Island Resort or Solomons Discovery Cruises tours.

Active conservation initiatives, ranked by impact and accessibility

Top 10 in Solomon Islands

  1. Tetepare Descendants Association. Community-led ranger patrols, leatherback turtle nesting protection, dugong monitoring, beche-de-mer (sea cucumber) surveys. Funded directly by Tetepare Eco Lodge revenue and external grants. Visitor engagement: ranger ride-alongs by community arrangement [9].
  2. Arnavon Community Marine Conservation Area (ACMCA). Hawksbill turtle nesting protection since 1995. Documented recovery of nesting beach numbers across three decades. Partner: The Nature Conservancy Solomon Islands country programme [8].
  3. Coral Sea Foundation. Reef monitoring across Western Province plus the Sea Women of Melanesia training programme. Cohorts of indigenous women trained in reef survey methodology and dive certification. Operates from Uepi Island Resort and Munda lodges [16].
  4. WWF Solomon Islands Western Province MPAs. Multi-village community managed area network in Vona Vona, Roviana, and surrounding Western Province lagoons [17].
  5. Marovo Lagoon community managed MPAs. Village-level reef management across the largest saltwater lagoon on Earth. Partners include Uepi Island Resort and the World Wildlife Fund Solomon Islands [11].
  6. Roviana Marine Conservation Programme. Community-led reef and turtle monitoring in Roviana and Vonavona Lagoons. Pairs with Munda lodges.
  7. WorldFish Solomon Islands. Auki, Malaita Province. Community fisheries management and household-scale food security research. Long-running CGIAR-aligned programme [18].
  8. SILMMA (Solomon Islands Locally Managed Marine Areas) network. National-level coordination across every community-managed site in the country. Anchored to Locally Managed Marine Area Network, regional HQ in Suva, Fiji.
  9. East Rennell management programme. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1998. Community management plan covers Lake Tegano (the largest enclosed lake in the South Pacific) and surrounding marine waters [10].
  10. KSLOF Global Reef Expedition data archive. Khaled bin Sultan Living Oceans Foundation surveys of 2014 produced the most comprehensive single-country reef baseline available. Publicly available open dataset [5].

Top 10 across adjacent Pacific and Indian Ocean

  1. Vatu-i-Ra Conservation Park, Fiji. Community-managed MPA in Bligh Water, established 2012, partners with Wildlife Conservation Society Fiji [19].
  2. Namena Marine Reserve, Fiji. Kubulau community-managed marine area, established 1997, Vanua Levu south coast [20].
  3. Aldabra Atoll UNESCO World Heritage, Seychelles. World's second-largest raised coral atoll, inscribed 1982, managed by Seychelles Islands Foundation [21].
  4. Hanifaru Bay UNESCO Biosphere, Maldives. Reef manta aggregation site within Baa Atoll Biosphere, inscribed 2011 [22]. See Manta Trust profile.
  5. Reefscapers Maldives. Coral propagation across 30+ resorts since 2001 [23]. See Reefscapers profile.
  6. Misool Foundation, Raja Ampat (Indonesia). 1,220 km² no-take zone managed by Misool Eco Resort and Foundation [24].
  7. Mahonia Na Dari Research and Conservation Centre, Kimbe Bay (PNG). Reef research and education NGO co-located with Walindi Plantation Resort [25].
  8. BIOT Marine Protected Area (Chagos). 640,000 km² no-take MPA gazetted 2010 [26].
  9. Save Our Seas Foundation D'Arros Research Centre, Seychelles. Long-running shark and ray research [27]. See SOSF profile.
  10. Bird's Head Seascape Initiative, Indonesia. Flagship Coral Triangle MPA network spanning Raja Ampat, Cenderawasih, and Triton Bay [28].

When to go: a season-by-season guide

April to June. The end of the wet season. Visibility improving, surface conditions calming. Manta ray sightings building in Marovo and Russell Islands. Hawksbill nesting peak at the Arnavons begins.

July to September. Peak dry season. Lowest rainfall, calmest seas, highest visibility (typically 25 to 40 metres). Best general diving window. Lodges run at higher capacity; book 4 to 6 months ahead.

October to early November. Shoulder season. Excellent conditions before the wet season returns. Lower lodge prices. Hammerhead schooling reports peak in Marovo and the Russell Islands.

Mid-November to February. Wet season and cyclone risk window. Reduced visibility (10 to 20 metres typical), more squally weather. Some lodges close or reduce capacity. Leatherback turtle nesting season begins on Tetepare in November and runs through February [9]. The trade-off: rougher conditions for the chance to walk a nesting beach with rangers.

March. Wet season tailing off. Conditions improving but still variable. Quieter, lower prices.

Getting there

International flights to Honiara (HIR). Solomon Airlines operates the primary connections from Brisbane, Sydney, Auckland, and Nadi (Fiji). Virgin Australia also serves the Brisbane to Honiara route. Flight times: Brisbane 3 hours, Sydney 4 hours via Brisbane, Auckland 4 hours direct, Nadi 3 hours.

From the United States. Best route is Los Angeles to Brisbane on Qantas, then Brisbane to Honiara. Total transit 18 to 22 hours.

From the United Kingdom or Europe. Heathrow to Brisbane via Singapore on Qantas or British Airways, then Brisbane to Honiara. Total transit 30 to 36 hours.

Internal logistics. Solomon Airlines operates daily flights from Honiara to Munda, Gizo, Buala, Auki, and other provincial airfields. Light aircraft charters via Solomon Airlines or specialist operators (e.g. Wings Across the Solomons) reach smaller airstrips. Inter-island travel within provinces is by motor canoe or small ferry.

Visa. Visitors from most Commonwealth countries, the European Union, the United States, Japan, and many others receive a 90-day visa on arrival. Confirm with Solomon Islands Immigration before travel.

Money. Solomon Islands Dollar (SBD). Exchange in Honiara. Cards accepted at Honiara hotels; lodges in Western and Isabel Provinces are largely cash-only. Bring Australian dollars or US dollars to convert.

Language. English official, Solomon Islands Pijin lingua franca. Provincial languages include Roviana, Marovo, Cheke Holo. A few Pijin phrases are appreciated; fluent English is standard at all lodges.

Insurance. Travel insurance with comprehensive medical evacuation cover is required. Honiara hospital is functional but provincial healthcare is limited. The platform requires evidence of insurance at booking.

Compared with adjacent destinations

Solomons vs Vanuatu for diving

Solomons has higher coral biodiversity and more diverse wreck inventory across multiple provinces. Vanuatu has the SS President Coolidge, the world's most-cited single wreck dive [29]. Choose Solomons for reef variety, Vanuatu for one defining wreck experience.

Solomons vs Fiji for luxury

Fiji offers more 5-star branded properties (Laucala, Kokomo, Royal Davui, Six Senses Fiji). Solomons offers a smaller, less-visited lodge tier with deeper community ownership. Fiji for branded ultra-luxury, Solomons for purpose-led travel.

Solomons vs Maldives for reef restoration

Maldives has the densest concentration of resort-led restoration via Reefscapers, Marine Savers, Soneva Foundation. Solomons restoration is community-led via Tetepare and Arnavon, channelling more dollars per visitor to indigenous management [8] [9] [23].

Solomons vs Raja Ampat for biodiversity

Raja Ampat is the global epicentre by raw species count (over 1,400 reef fish documented). Solomons sits in the second tier alongside Wakatobi and parts of PNG. Raja Ampat for marquee numbers, Solomons for fewer divers per dive site [4].

Solomons vs Palau for shark conservation

Palau is the world reference for national-scale shark sanctuary policy with its 500,000 km² Palau National Marine Sanctuary [30]. Solomons has community-managed protections at Arnavon and other sites but no equivalent national no-take. Palau for sanctuary policy, Solomons for grassroots community-managed model.

The future and outlook

Solomon Islands government policy has signalled accelerating MPA expansion through the SILMMA network and the National Ocean Policy [31]. The country's commitment under the 30 by 30 framework (30 percent of national waters protected by 2030) is one of several Pacific Island state pledges to expand marine area coverage substantially over the coming decade.

Climate adaptation is the central strategic challenge. Sea-level rise and coral bleaching are not problems that domestic Solomon Islands action alone can solve; the country's adaptation strategy depends on global emissions reductions plus targeted resilience investment. Restoration alone cannot keep pace with climate-driven loss; the realistic role of restoration is to supplement mitigation, maintain reef function locally, and preserve community livelihood while broader policy works.

Blue economy initiatives are emerging. The Solomon Islands joined the Blue Pacific Continent framework alongside other Pacific Islands Forum members. Coastal-zone management plans for high-priority lagoons (Marovo, Roviana, Vona Vona) are in development with support from SPC, SPREP, and bilateral partners [32].

The premium-eco tourism opportunity over the next five years is to grow capacity in places that genuinely lack it. Choiseul, Temotu, Rennell-Bellona, and parts of Malaita have substantial conservation programmes but very limited tourism infrastructure. The platform's longer-term strategy includes helping owner-operators in those places build the right kind of property at the right scale.

Long-tail FAQ

The questions below capture the most common queries we have observed about Solomon Islands eco tourism. Answers are sourced from primary materials throughout this page; see Sources for citations.

Is the Solomon Islands safe for tourists?
Yes for most premium-eco travel. Honiara has petty crime concerns at night; the Western Province lodges (Marovo, Munda, Gizo) are calm and well-policed by their own communities. The UK FCDO and Australian Smartraveller advisories rate the country as broadly safe with sensible precautions.
What is the best month to visit the Solomon Islands for diving?
April to November. Visibility is highest from June to October. October offers the best balance of low rainfall, high visibility, and shoulder pricing. The cyclone season runs December to March.
Can I volunteer with marine conservation in the Solomon Islands?
Yes. Tetepare Descendants Association accepts ranger-aligned visitors. Arnavon CMCA accepts research-aligned visitors during hawksbill nesting season. Coral Sea Foundation Sea Women of Melanesia cohorts offer visitor support roles. Each requires advance application.
Which Solomon Islands resort has the best house reef?
Uepi Island Resort in Marovo Lagoon. The drop-off begins approximately 30 metres from the beach and reaches over 800 metres in the deepest section. Hammerheads, dogtooth tuna, manta ray, and grey reef shark are routine.
How does eco-tourism help conservation in the Solomon Islands?
Premium-eco bookings finance ranger salaries, turtle nesting protection, reef monitoring, and community managed marine areas. Tetepare's ranger programme is funded directly by eco lodge revenue; Uepi funds reef monitoring with Coral Sea Foundation; WWF Solomons and TNC Arnavon programmes are partly underwritten by visitor fees.
What is the cheapest way to dive in the Solomon Islands?
Munda. Dive Munda offers boat dives in Roviana Lagoon and Munda Bay at price points well below resort dive operations. Munda Coastal Lodge or Agnes Gateway Hotel provide accommodation that pairs cleanly. Expect roughly 40 to 60 percent less than equivalent resort packages.
Can I see manta rays in the Solomon Islands?
Yes. Marovo Lagoon and the Russell Islands have documented manta sightings, especially May to October. Munda Bay also has sightings. The Solomons are not a Hanifaru Bay equivalent but mantas are reliably present at multiple Western Province sites.
Which lodge is best for families with children?
Tavanipupu Island Resort in Marau Sound has historically been the family choice. Sanbis on Lola Island accepts children with shallow reef snorkelling. Uepi has age-12-plus diving programmes for older children.
Are there liveaboards in the Solomon Islands?
Yes. The MV Bilikiki and MV Spirit of Solomons are the two longest-running purpose-built dive liveaboards. Both work the Russell Islands, Marovo, and Florida Islands routes. Trips run roughly April to November.
How long does it take to fly to the Solomon Islands?
From Brisbane: 3 hours direct. From Sydney: 4 hours via Brisbane. From Auckland: 4 hours direct. From the US: 18 to 22 hours via Brisbane. From the UK: 30 to 36 hours via Brisbane or Singapore.
Can I see leatherback turtles in the Solomon Islands?
Yes, on Tetepare Island during the November to February nesting season. The TDA rangers monitor nesting beaches. Visitor access is by community arrangement only. Hawksbill nesting is more reliable in the Arnavon Islands.
Solomon Islands vs Vanuatu for diving?
Solomons has higher coral biodiversity and more diverse wreck diving across multiple provinces. Vanuatu has the SS President Coolidge, unmatched globally as a single-wreck dive. Solomons for variety; Vanuatu for one iconic wreck.
Solomon Islands vs Fiji for luxury?
Fiji has more 5-star branded properties. Solomons offers a smaller, more authentic eco lodge tier with significantly lower visitor density. Fiji for branded ultra-luxury, Solomons for unspoiled Coral Triangle reefs.
Solomon Islands vs Maldives for reef restoration impact?
Maldives has the densest concentration of resort-led restoration. Solomons restoration is community-led. The Solomons model channels more dollars per visitor to indigenous community management; Maldives produces more measurable coral propagation outputs.
Solomon Islands vs Raja Ampat for biodiversity?
Both sit in the Coral Triangle. Raja Ampat is the global epicentre by raw species count. Solomons is second tier alongside Wakatobi and PNG. Raja Ampat for marquee numbers, Solomons for fewer divers per site.
Solomon Islands vs Palau for shark conservation?
Palau is the world reference for national-scale shark sanctuary policy. Solomons has community-managed protections at Arnavon and other sites but no equivalent national no-take. Palau for sanctuary policy, Solomons for community-managed model.
What languages are spoken in the Solomon Islands?
English is the official language. Solomon Islands Pijin is the lingua franca. Over 70 indigenous languages are spoken. Lodges and dive operators are fluent in English.
Do I need a visa for the Solomon Islands?
Visitors from most Commonwealth countries, the EU, the United States, Japan, and others receive 90-day visa-free entry on arrival. Confirm with Solomon Islands Immigration before travel.
What currency is used in the Solomon Islands?
Solomon Islands Dollar (SBD). Honiara has ATMs and cards are accepted at hotels. Lodges in Western and Isabel Provinces are largely cash-only.
Marovo Lagoon eco lodge: which one?
Uepi Island Resort is the established premium-eco choice on the Marovo barrier reef. Several village-based homestays operate within the Marovo Lagoon UNESCO Tentative World Heritage area for lower cost.
