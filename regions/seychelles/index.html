Region reference, Indian Ocean

Seychelles: lodges, Aldabra UNESCO, and Save Our Seas.

Seven premium-eco lodges across the Inner Islands, the Aldabra Atoll UNESCO World Heritage Site (1982), and the Save Our Seas Foundation D'Arros Research Centre in the Amirantes.

The short version. The Seychelles is a 115-island Western Indian Ocean archipelago. The Inner Islands cluster (Mahé, Praslin, La Digue + smaller granitic islands) hosts the premium-eco lodges. The Outer Islands (Amirantes, Aldabra group) host the most globally significant marine science. Aldabra is the world's second-largest raised coral atoll and one of just a few intact giant tortoise habitats remaining anywhere [1].

Inner Islands premium-eco lodges

North Island Seychelles

Eleven private villas across a granite-and-beach private island. Sister-aligned with Islands Development Company restoration of native flora and fauna, including the Aldabra giant tortoise reintroduction [2].

Frégate Island Private

Sixteen villas across a 219-hectare granite island. Long-running magpie robin reintroduction programme, plus active hawksbill turtle nesting research with the Seychelles Islands Foundation [3].

Six Senses Zil Pasyon (Félicité Island)

Thirty pool villas on Félicité Island, just east of La Digue. Sister-aligned with Six Senses Earth Lab programmes [4].

Cousine Island

Four ultra-private villas on a 27-hectare granite island that doubles as a Special Reserve under Seychelles law. Long-running endemic bird and turtle conservation [5].

Denis Private Island

Twenty-five rustic-luxury cottages on a coral cay 95 km north of Mahé. Major Magpie Robin and Seychelles paradise flycatcher reintroduction work plus turtle nesting protection [6].

Bird Island Lodge

Twenty-four eco bungalows on a coral cay famous for sooty tern colony numbering several hundred thousand pairs in the breeding season (April to October) [7].

Constance Lemuria Resort, Praslin

5-star beachfront resort, the only golf course in Seychelles. Hawksbill turtle nesting protection programme on Anse Georgette beach [8].

Aldabra Atoll UNESCO World Heritage

Aldabra is the world's second-largest raised coral atoll, gazetted as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1982 [1]. It is one of the most isolated places on Earth and is managed by the Seychelles Islands Foundation under government concession. Aldabra hosts approximately 100,000 Aldabra giant tortoises (Aldabrachelys gigantea), the world's largest population [9]. Visitor access is severely restricted; only the Aldabra Research Station permits crewed yacht visits.

Outer Islands restoration: D'Arros and IDC

SOSF D'Arros Research Centre

D'Arros Island and St Joseph Atoll, in the Amirantes, host the Save Our Seas Foundation D'Arros Research Centre, focused on shark, ray, and reef research. Long-running tagging studies on lemon and grey reef sharks [10]. See the dedicated Save Our Seas profile.

Islands Development Company (IDC)

IDC is the para-statal company that manages economic activity on most of the Seychelles Outer Islands under government concession. Their restoration arm focuses on coconut palm replanting, native habitat restoration, and ranger-supported turtle nesting protection across the Amirantes and Aldabra group [11].

Honest gaps

  • Reef bleaching. The Seychelles experienced major bleaching in 1998 and 2016 with the Inner Islands suffering 50% to 90% live coral cover loss in shallow zones [12]. Restoration cannot solve climate.
  • Outer Islands access. Aldabra, the Cosmoledo group, and Astove are reachable only by chartered yacht or research vessel; commercial accommodation is limited to expedition charters.
